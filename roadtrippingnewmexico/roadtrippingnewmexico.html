Roadtripping New Mexico

From the vast open stretches of De-Na-Zin Wilderness Area to the beautiful open views of Tent Rocks National Monument and the amazing desert of White Sands National Monument, here's how to spend 7 amazing days exploring the nation's fifth-largest state.

Day 1: Albuquerque, New Mexico

Why go:

Albuquerque is the perfect gateway to all the art, nature and culture the state has to offer. I originally took this trip in just five days so I didn't have time to fully explore the area, but it's home to the balloon museum, the biopark and more than 100 art galleries.

Day 1-2: Bisti / De-Na-Zin Wilderness Area

Why go:

Three hours northwest of Albuquerque lies some of the most unique landscapes found between Mars and Venus. Bisti is so open and wild that there are no trails. It's easy to feel like you've somehow gotten lost on another planet, making it the perfect place for any adventurous soul looking for an out-of-this-world hiking and camping experience.

Day 2: Chaco National Historical Monument

Why go:

As far as dirt "road" (path?) detours go, Chaco Canyon is a memorable one. The park's architecture dates all the way back to before 900BC. Villages started forming in the canyon around 200AD the site became a major gathering place for the Puebloan people around 850AD. It's the definition of a time trip.

Day 3: Bandelier National Monument

Why go:

On the way to Santa Fe, Bandelier's 70 miles of hiking trails offer a great opportunity to stretch the legs — no matter your fitness level. I hiked the easy 1.2 mile Main Loop Trail and the 1 mile Alcove House which included a voluntary but adventurously high ladder climb to see the view from Ceremonial Cave.

Day 3-4: Santa Fe, New Mexico

Why go:

Art, art, art, and more art. Did I mention art?! From the crazy-insane, mind-bending story unfolding within the innovative installations at Meow Wolf to the wildly inspiring works found in the Georgia O'Keefe Museum and the beautiful works of the Museum of Encaustic Art — or any of the other 250 galleries and studios open to the public — Santa Fe is a true art mecca — and the reason why the wild west remains so closely tied to the American imagination.

Day 4: Tent Rocks National Monument

Why go:

Located just an hour from Santa Fe, skipping out on Tent Rocks would be a major mistake for any nature lover to make. I hiked the 3-mile Canyon Trail, traversing through a slot canyon and a beautiful 630-foot climb up through the park's large hoodoos for a gorgeous view of the Sandia Mountains and the Rio Grande Valley. As far as I can tell, the trail's Difficulty to Payoff ratio is one of the best in the west.

Day 5: Carlsbad Caverns National Park

Why go:

So far in New Mexico, we've traveled through space (Bisti), time (Chaco) and mind (Santa Fe) — all that's left is to head underground! I hiked down the super steep Natural Entrance Trail but there's an elevator for anyone pressed for time or stamina. Down below is an extravagantly carved limestone castle made by an inland sea 265 million years ago, showing that nature really is one of the world's most amazing artists.

Day 6: White Sands National Monument

Why go:

Going to New Mexico without going to White Sands would be like going to the beach without seeing the water. White Sands, as the world's largest gypsum dunefield, is truly a natural wonder. The 275 square miles of phenomenal dunes seemingly rise from nowhere, and though the mountains beyond them remain just about the same over decades, the dunes are constantly changing and transforming. While most visiters stay near the main causeway to go sand sledding, the dunes provide stunning hiking opportunities, like the 5-mile-loop Alkali Flat Trail. (Feel free to turn around at any time — you'll definitely wish there was water around.)
